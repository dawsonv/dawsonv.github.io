About Me

The social forces that shape human life fascinate me, from the structure of cities to the determinants of development. Within this diverse set of interests, my primary focus is on refugee resettlement and education policy. I've conducted award-winning quantitative research, done advocacy, and worked in classrooms, all to shed light on an experience that can sometimes seem invisible: a lost generation of students caught up in conflict. In the end, because the foundations of morality lie in our others, I view research in the social sciences as an instrument of morality – a tool that can be used to look out for vulnerable populations and diffuse the challenges they face. The world is filled with systematic injustices, within and between countries. Some are born into horrific circumstances through no fault of thier own. It's the role of the policy maker, the researcher, and the civil servant alike to reduce that injustice.

That brings me to the title of this website. Complex social problems require manifold, carefully tuned policy regimes. To allow uninformed ideology to guide policy with no less than lives at stake is no less than neglligent. Instead, careful analysis and open debate must guide inform political action. I find that my hopes are best expressed by a Chinese proverb: "let one hundred flowers bloom; let one hundred schools of thought contend". For a fleeting moment, this ideology was reflected by the Hundred Flowers Campaign. In an effort to enfranchise intilectuals, Mao Zedong announced in May of 1956 that open critiscism of the communist party and its policies would be allowed.

"[Let] a hundred flowers bloom and a hundred schools of thought contend" – Mao Zedong

Of course, the liberal spirit of the Hundred Flowers Campaign never came to fruition. Chinese history is definied by periods of liberalization punctuated by violent regression. In July of the following year, party leaders launched an antirightists' campaign. To signal the end of an era, recent critics of the communist party were forced into manual labor or prison.

Needless to say, the outcome of the Hundred Flowers Campaign was deeply regrettable.
